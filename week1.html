Summary of the week
During the first week we spent most of the time in individually researching topics of interest. Towards the end of the week we agreed on starting this journey together. First we researched quite freely in body augmentation projects, mainly in the speculative realm. We wanted to avoid fashion/tattoos. Then we started to think on whether there was a connection in the way western/eastern folks design augmented devices for their bodies, starting from an historical phylosophical perspective. Eventually we decided to narrow down to a specifi context, the bath house and bathing rituals. Eventually we attended the first crit with all the tutors.

Research on Body Augmentation

What is body augmentation? Can we find different categories to differentiate different kinds of augmentation? These were the first questions that we asked ourselves. Below is the definition according to technopedia.

Human augmentation is generally used to refer to technologies that enhance human productivity or capability, or that somehow add to the human body. Modern advancements in many areas of IT have led to a greater variety of implants and other technologies that could be classed as human augmentation. Human augmentation may also be called human 2.0.

Bath Culture

Given the fact that we are supposed to deliver a project within 5 weeks, we thought of narrowing down to the topic of bathing rituals in order to plan the construction of a feasible prototype. In bathing rituals, different cultures look at the body in specific ways. We are not so keen on architecture of steam houses as we are interested in the props that facilitate the rituals of entering such spaces. We run into this fantastic magazine called Wet, which for a couple of months documented bathing cultures in a glamour way. Then we also looked at the exhibition "Soak, Steam, Dream" that took place here in London until few weeks ago.

Cool cover pages of the magazine Wet.
